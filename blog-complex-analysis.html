Closed Is Not Exact: From Stokes' Theorem to Cauchy

By Ram Prakash | December 28, 2025 | Category: Complex Analysis

The three volumes of The Feynman Lectures on Physics are very popular among physicists and are widely considered the bible of modern physics. In The Feynman Lectures on Physics, Volume II: Mainly Electromagnetism and Matter, in the second chapter, the author remarks:

"The interesting theorem is that if the curl of \(\mathbf{A}\) is zero, then \(\mathbf{A}\) is always the gradient of something—there is some scalar field \(\psi\) such that \(\mathbf{A} = \nabla \psi\). In other words, we have the theorem:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf{A} = \nabla \psi \tag{2.50}$$

At first glance, this might seem like a harmless technicality, but it's not. The statement \(\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = 0 \Rightarrow \mathbf{A} = \nabla \psi\) is false in general, and understanding why is crucial for anyone working with vector fields, whether in physics or mathematics. The error lies in overlooking the topology of the domain. In domains with holes (like the region around a solenoid, or the space \(\mathbb{R}^3 \setminus \{z\text{-axis}\}\) in electromagnetic problems), there exist vector fields with zero curl that are not gradients of any scalar function.

What makes this particularly serious is that such "topological obstructions" appear everywhere in physics: from the Aharonov-Bohm effect to magnetic monopoles, from fluid dynamics around vortices to electromagnetic theory in non-simply-connected regions. Ignoring the topology can lead to incorrect physical predictions and flawed mathematical reasoning. The "Feynman mistake" is therefore not just a mathematical footnote, rather it's a fundamental warning that domain geometry matters.

In this blog, I aim to share some insights into the relation between complex analysis and differential topology, and explain why this mistake should not be ignored.

Setting the Stage

Let \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{C}\) be an open set, and let \(f : \Omega \to \mathbb{C}\) be a holomorphic function. Before we discuss primitives, let's understand what holomorphic means: a function is holomorphic if it is complex differentiable at every point in its domain, meaning the limit

$$f'(z_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0)}{h}$$

exists and is the same regardless of how \(h\) approaches 0 in the complex plane.

Local vs Global Primitives

Definition: Local Primitive

We say that \(f\) admits a primitive at a point \(z_0 \in \Omega\) if there exists an open neighborhood \(U \subset \Omega\) with \(z_0 \in U\) and a holomorphic function \(F : U \to \mathbb{C}\) such that \(F'(z) = f(z)\) for all \(z \in U\).

Definition: Global Primitive

We say that \(f\) admits a (global) primitive on \(\Omega\) if there exists a holomorphic function \(F : \Omega \to \mathbb{C}\) satisfying \(F'(z) = f(z)\) for all \(z \in \Omega\). In this case, the function \(F\) is called a primitive (or antiderivative) of \(f\) on \(\Omega\).

The Key Distinction

The distinction between local and global primitives is crucial in complex analysis. Every holomorphic function admits a local primitive at every point of its domain – this follows from the existence of power series expansions. However, the existence of a global primitive is a much more subtle question that depends on the geometry of the domain \(\Omega\).

Primitives and Integrals: The Fundamental Connection

If \(f\) has a global primitive, then integrals vanish

Suppose \(f\) admits a global primitive \(F\) on \(\Omega\). Then for any closed curve \(\gamma : [a,b] \to \Omega\) (where \(\gamma(a) = \gamma(b)\)), we have:

$$\oint_\gamma f(z) \, dz = \int_a^b f(\gamma(t)) \gamma'(t) \, dt = \int_a^b F'(\gamma(t)) \gamma'(t) \, dt$$

By the chain rule, this becomes:

$$\oint_\gamma f(z) \, dz = \int_a^b \frac{d}{dt}[F(\gamma(t))] \, dt = F(\gamma(b)) - F(\gamma(a)) = 0$$

since \(\gamma(a) = \gamma(b)\). This is a direct consequence of the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus applied to complex functions.

Path independence and the primitive

Moreover, if \(F\) is a global primitive of \(f\), then the integral of \(f\) between any two points \(z_1, z_2 \in \Omega\) can be expressed as \(\int_\gamma f(z) \, dz = F(z_2) - F(z_1)\), where \(\gamma\) is any path in \(\Omega\) from \(z_1\) to \(z_2\). This shows that the integral is path-independent – it depends only on the endpoints, not on the specific curve chosen. This is a remarkable property that distinguishes functions with global primitives.

The converse: when vanishing integrals imply existence of primitives

The converse is equally profound: if a holomorphic function \(f\) satisfies \(\oint_\gamma f(z) \, dz = 0\) for every closed curve \(\gamma\) in \(\Omega\), then \(f\) admits a global primitive on \(\Omega\). The construction of such a primitive proceeds as follows: fix a point \(z_0 \in \Omega\) and define for any \(z \in \Omega\):

$$F(z) = \int_{\gamma_z} f(w) \, dw$$

where \(\gamma_z\) is any curve in \(\Omega\) from \(z_0\) to \(z\). The condition that all closed integrals vanish guarantees that this definition is independent of the choice of curve \(\gamma_z\), making \(F\) well-defined. One can then verify that \(F'(z) = f(z)\) for all \(z \in \Omega\).

A Special Case: Star-Shaped Domains

An important class of domains where every holomorphic function admits a primitive is the star-shaped domains. A domain \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{C}\) is called star-shaped (with respect to a point \(z_0 \in \Omega\)) if for every point \(z \in \Omega\), the entire line segment from \(z_0\) to \(z\) lies within \(\Omega\): \(\{z_0 + t(z - z_0) : t \in [0,1]\} \subset \Omega\) for all \(z \in \Omega\).

Theorem: If \(\Omega\) is a star-shaped domain and \(f : \Omega \to \mathbb{C}\) is holomorphic, then \(f\) admits a primitive on \(\Omega\).

Proof: Fix the "star center" \(z_0 \in \Omega\). For any \(z \in \Omega\), define \(F(z) = \int_{[z_0,z]} f(w) \, dw\), where \([z_0,z]\) denotes the straight line segment from \(z_0\) to \(z\) (which lies in \(\Omega\) by star-shapedness). By differentiating under the integral sign or using a direct argument with the definition of the derivative, one can show \(F'(z) = f(z)\).

Star-shaped domains are more general than simply connected domains (convex domains are a special case of star-shaped domains, where any point can serve as the star center). This result guarantees that on such domains, the integral of any holomorphic function around any closed curve is zero.

When Do Global Primitives Exist?

Combining these results, we have the fundamental characterization: a holomorphic function \(f\) admits a global primitive on \(\Omega\) if and only if the integral of \(f\) around every closed curve in \(\Omega\) is zero.

This condition is intimately related to the topology of the domain \(\Omega\). Simply connected domains (domains without "holes") always satisfy this property for holomorphic functions, guaranteeing the existence of global primitives.

Example: The function \(f(z) = \frac{1}{z}\) is holomorphic on \(\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}\), but it does not admit a global primitive on this domain because the integral around the unit circle is \(2\pi i \neq 0\). However, on any simply connected subdomain (like a slit plane), a primitive exists: \(F(z) = \log(z)\).

Geometric Interpretation

The existence of primitives connects complex analysis to the geometry of the underlying domain. Domains with holes (multiply connected domains) can prevent globally defined primitives from existing, even though local primitives always exist. This beautiful interplay between analysis and geometry is one of the reasons complex analysis is such a powerful and elegant subject. To understand it deeply, we need to understand differential forms.

Setup: Differential Forms and the Exterior Derivative

Let \((x^1, \dots, x^n)\) be coordinates on \(\mathbb{R}^n\). A differential \(k\)-form is an expression of the form:

$$\omega = \sum_{i_1 < \cdots < i_k} f_{i_1\cdots i_k}(x)\, dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}$$

where the wedge product \(\wedge\) is bilinear and alternating:

$$dx^i \wedge dx^j = - dx^j \wedge dx^i, \qquad dx^i \wedge dx^i = 0$$

The exterior derivative is an operator \(d : \Omega^k(\mathbb{R}^n) \to \Omega^{k+1}(\mathbb{R}^n)\) defined on a \(k\)-form \(\omega\) by:

$$d\omega = \sum_{i_1 < \cdots < i_k} df_{i_1\cdots i_k} \wedge dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}$$

where for a function \(f\), \(df = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^j}\,dx^j\).

The Fundamental Identity \(d^2 = 0\)

The identity \(d^2 = 0\) is one of the most important facts in differential geometry. It states that the exterior derivative applied twice always gives zero. This single identity contains within it the classical vector calculus identities \(\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0\) and \(\nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}\).

Connection to Vector Calculus

In \(\mathbb{R}^3\), this identity contains the classical divergence and curl identities. To see this, we identify:

  • 0-forms: functions \(f\)
  • 1-forms: correspond to vector fields via \(\omega = f_1\,dx + f_2\,dy + f_3\,dz \leftrightarrow \mathbf{F} = (f_1, f_2, f_3)\)
  • 2-forms: correspond to vector fields via \(\omega = g_1\,dy \wedge dz + g_2\,dz \wedge dx + g_3\,dx \wedge dy \leftrightarrow \mathbf{G} = (g_1, g_2, g_3)\)

The curl identity \(\nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}\): For a \(0\)-form \(f\),

$$d^2 f = d\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\,dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\,dy + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\,dz\right) = \left(\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} - \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}\right)dx \wedge dy + \cdots = 0$$

This is \(\nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}\) in disguise.

The divergence identity \(\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0\): For a \(1\)-form \(\omega = f_1\,dx + f_2\,dy + f_3\,dz\),

$$d\omega = \left(\frac{\partial f_3}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial z}\right)dy \wedge dz + \left(\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial f_3}{\partial x}\right)dz \wedge dx + \left(\frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial y}\right)dx \wedge dy$$

Then \(d^2\omega = 0\) gives three equations, one of which is:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(\frac{\partial f_3}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial f_2}{\partial z}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\!\left(\frac{\partial f_1}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial f_3}{\partial x}\right) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\!\left(\frac{\partial f_2}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial f_1}{\partial y}\right) = 0$$

This is exactly \(\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0\) where \(\mathbf{F} = (f_1, f_2, f_3)\).

The identity \(d^2 = 0\) is fundamental. It leads us directly to two of the most important concepts in differential geometry and topology: closed forms and exact forms.

Closed and Exact Forms

Definition: Closed and Exact Forms

A differential \(k\)-form \(\omega\) is called closed if \(d\omega = 0\).

A differential \(k\)-form \(\omega\) is called exact if there exists a \((k-1)\)-form \(\eta\) such that \(\omega = d\eta\).

The identity \(d^2 = 0\) immediately tells us that every exact form is closed: if \(\omega = d\eta\), then \(d\omega = d(d\eta) = d^2\eta = 0\). The converse question—is every closed form exact?—is where topology enters the picture in a profound way.

In vector calculus language, this question takes on a familiar form:

  • For 0-forms (functions): If \(\nabla f = \mathbf{0}\), is \(f\) constant? (Yes, on connected domains)
  • For 1-forms (vector fields): If \(\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{0}\), does there exist a potential \(\psi\) with \(\mathbf{A} = \nabla \psi\)?
  • For 2-forms (vector fields in \(\mathbb{R}^3\)): If \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\), does there exist a vector potential \(\mathbf{A}\) with \(\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}\)?

The middle question is precisely the one Feynman got wrong! The statement "\(\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{0} \Rightarrow \mathbf{A} = \nabla \psi\)" is equivalent to saying "every closed 1-form is exact," and this depends on the topology of the domain.

Poincaré Lemma: On a simply connected domain (a domain without holes), every closed form is exact. In particular, if \(\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n\) is star-shaped (a special case of simply connected), then:

  • If \(\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{0}\), then \(\mathbf{A} = \nabla \psi\) for some potential \(\psi\)
  • If \(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0\), then \(\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}\) for some vector potential \(\mathbf{A}\)

The Poincaré Lemma fails when the domain has holes!

The Counterexample: Why Domain Topology Matters

Consider the domain \(\Omega = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{(0,0)\}\) (the plane with the origin removed). The 1-form

$$\omega = \frac{-y\,dx + x\,dy}{x^2 + y^2}$$

is closed (you can verify that \(d\omega = 0\)), but it is not exact on \(\Omega\)! If we integrate \(\omega\) around the unit circle \(\gamma(t) = (\cos t, \sin t)\):

$$\oint_\gamma \omega = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{-\sin t(-\sin t) + \cos t(\cos t)}{\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t}\,dt = \int_0^{2\pi} dt = 2\pi \neq 0$$

If \(\omega\) were exact, say \(\omega = d\eta\), then by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, \(\oint_\gamma \omega = \eta(\gamma(2\pi)) - \eta(\gamma(0)) = 0\) since \(\gamma\) is a closed curve. The non-zero integral proves \(\omega\) cannot be exact.

In vector calculus notation, this 1-form corresponds to the vector field:

$$\mathbf{A} = \left(\frac{-y}{x^2 + y^2}, \frac{x}{x^2 + y^2}\right)$$

which satisfies \(\nabla \times \mathbf{A} = 0\) everywhere in \(\Omega\), yet \(\mathbf{A}\) is not the gradient of any scalar function. This is the precise counterexample to Feynman's claim!

The Connection to \(f(z) = \frac{1}{z}\) and Cauchy-Riemann Equations

Remarkably, the counterexample \(\omega = \frac{-y\,dx + x\,dy}{x^2 + y^2}\) is exactly the imaginary part of the complex differential form \(\frac{1}{z}\,dz\)! Let's see this connection explicitly.

Write \(z = x + iy\) and \(dz = dx + i\,dy\). Then:

$$\frac{1}{z}\,dz = \frac{1}{x+iy}(dx + i\,dy) = \frac{x-iy}{x^2+y^2}(dx + i\,dy)$$

Multiplying out and separating real and imaginary parts:

$$\frac{1}{z}\,dz = \frac{x\,dx + y\,dy}{x^2+y^2} + i\,\frac{-y\,dx + x\,dy}{x^2+y^2}$$

The imaginary part is precisely our 1-form \(\omega = \frac{-y\,dx + x\,dy}{x^2+y^2}\)! So when we compute \(\oint_\gamma \frac{dz}{z}\) around the unit circle:

$$\oint_\gamma \frac{dz}{z} = \oint_\gamma \frac{x\,dx + y\,dy}{x^2+y^2} + i\oint_\gamma \frac{-y\,dx + x\,dy}{x^2+y^2} = 0 + i(2\pi) = 2\pi i$$

The real part vanishes (it's exact: \(d(\frac{1}{2}\log(x^2+y^2))\)), but the imaginary part gives \(2\pi\)—the obstruction to having a primitive!

The Cauchy-Riemann Connection

Write \(f(z) = \frac{1}{z} = u(x,y) + i\,v(x,y)\) where \(u = \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\) and \(v = \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}\).

The Cauchy-Riemann equations are:

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}, \qquad \frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}$$

You can verify these hold everywhere except \((0,0)\)! This confirms \(\frac{1}{z}\) is holomorphic on \(\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}\).

Now let's understand the deep connection between the Cauchy-Riemann equations and 2D differential forms. For any complex function \(f = u + iv\), the complex differential \(f(z)\,dz\) decomposes into two real 1-forms:

$$f(z)\,dz = (u+iv)(dx+i\,dy) = \underbrace{(u\,dx - v\,dy)}_{\text{real part}} + i\underbrace{(v\,dx + u\,dy)}_{\text{imaginary part}}$$

Let's compute the exterior derivative of the imaginary part \(\omega_2 = v\,dx + u\,dy\):

$$d\omega_2 = d(v\,dx + u\,dy) = \left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\,dx + \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\,dy\right) \wedge dx + \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\,dx + \frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\,dy\right) \wedge dy$$

Using the fact that \(dx \wedge dx = dy \wedge dy = 0\) and \(dy \wedge dx = -dx \wedge dy\), we get:

$$d\omega_2 = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\,dy \wedge dx + \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\,dx \wedge dy = \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\right)dx \wedge dy$$

The Cauchy-Riemann equation \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial v}{\partial y}\) makes \(d\omega_2 = 0\!

Similarly, for the real part \(\omega_1 = u\,dx - v\,dy\):

$$d\omega_1 = d(u\,dx - v\,dy) = \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\right)dx \wedge dy$$

The second Cauchy-Riemann equation \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial v}{\partial x}\) makes \(d\omega_1 = 0\!

Key Insight: The Cauchy-Riemann equations are equivalent to saying that both the real and imaginary parts of \(f(z)\,dz\) are closed 1-forms! This is the profound link between complex analysis and differential forms.

For \(f(z) = \frac{1}{z}\), both \(\omega_1 = \frac{x\,dx + y\,dy}{x^2+y^2}\) and \(\omega_2 = \frac{-y\,dx + x\,dy}{x^2+y^2}\) are closed (you can verify this directly). However:

  • \(\omega_1\) is exact: \(\omega_1 = d\!\left(\frac{1}{2}\log(x^2+y^2)\right)\)
  • \(\omega_2\) is NOT exact on \(\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{(0,0)\}\): \(\oint_\gamma \omega_2 = 2\pi \neq 0\)

This asymmetry is crucial! The real part has a primitive (\(\frac{1}{2}\log(x^2+y^2) = \log|z|\)), but the imaginary part does not. The imaginary part would be \(d(\arg z) = d\theta\), but the angle function \(\theta\) is not single-valued on \(\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{(0,0)\}\)—as you go around the origin once, \(\theta\) increases by \(2\pi\)!

In complex notation, this is saying:

$$\frac{dz}{z} = d(\log|z|) + i\,d(\arg z)$$

The term \(d(\log|z|)\) is well-defined (it's exact), but \(d(\arg z)\) is not the differential of a single-valued function on the punctured plane. This is why \(\frac{1}{z}\) has no primitive—there's no way to define \(\log(z) = \log|z| + i\arg(z)\) as a single-valued holomorphic function on \(\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}\)!

The Fundamental Obstruction: The non-exactness of \(\omega_2 = \frac{-y\,dx + x\,dy}{x^2+y^2}\) is precisely why \(\log(z)\) can't be defined as a single-valued function. You need to introduce a "branch cut" (remove a ray from the origin) to make \(\arg(z)\) single-valued. On any simply connected subdomain (like the slit plane \(\mathbb{C} \setminus (-\infty,0]\)), \(\frac{1}{z}\) does have a primitive: \(\log(z)\)!

This beautifully demonstrates the Poincaré Lemma: locally, closed forms are exact. At any point \(z_0 \neq 0\), you can always find a small neighborhood where \(\frac{1}{z} = \frac{d}{dz}\log(z)\). But globally on \(\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}\), the hole at the origin prevents these local primitives from patching together into a single global primitive.

To summarize the chain of reasoning:

  1. Cauchy-Riemann equations \(\iff\) Both parts of \(f(z)\,dz\) are closed
  2. Closed does not imply exact on non-simply connected domains
  3. \(\omega_2\) is closed but not exact \(\iff\) \(\oint_\gamma \omega_2 \neq 0\) \(\iff\) \(\oint_\gamma \frac{dz}{z} \neq 0\)
  4. \(\oint_\gamma \frac{dz}{z} \neq 0\) \(\iff\) \(\frac{1}{z}\) has no primitive

Thus, the topological obstruction (the hole at the origin) manifests as:

  • A closed 1-form that isn't exact (\(\omega_2\))
  • A holomorphic function without a primitive (\(\frac{1}{z}\))
  • A curl-free vector field that isn't a gradient (\(\mathbf{A}\))

If \(\frac{1}{z}\) had a primitive \(F(z)\) on \(\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}\), then by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus for complex functions:

$$\oint_\gamma \frac{dz}{z} = F(\gamma(2\pi)) - F(\gamma(0)) = 0$$

for any closed curve \(\gamma\). But \(\oint_{|z|=1} \frac{dz}{z} = 2\pi i \neq 0\), providing a direct contradiction. The non-existence of a primitive for \(\frac{1}{z}\) is equivalent to the fact that our 1-form \(\omega\) is closed but not exact.

This beautiful connection shows that:

  • Complex analysis: \(\frac{1}{z}\) has no primitive on \(\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}\)
  • Differential forms: \(\omega = \frac{-y\,dx + x\,dy}{x^2+y^2}\) is closed but not exact
  • Vector calculus: \(\mathbf{A} = \left(\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}, \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right)\) has zero curl but is not a gradient

These are three manifestations of the same topological obstruction—the hole at the origin!

Conclusion

The study of primitives in complex analysis reveals the deep connection between the analytic properties of functions and the topological properties of their domains. From local existence guaranteed by power series to global existence dictated by geometry, this topic exemplifies the unity of mathematics.

Appendix: The Fundamental Identity \(d^2 = 0\)

Theorem: \(d^2 = 0\)

Proof

Let \(\omega = \sum_{i_1 < \cdots < i_k} f_{i_1\cdots i_k}\, dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}\). First compute:

$$d\omega = \sum_{i_1 < \cdots < i_k} \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial f_{i_1\cdots i_k}}{\partial x^j}\, dx^j \wedge dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}$$

Applying \(d\) again:

$$d(d\omega) = \sum_{i_1 < \cdots < i_k} \sum_{j=1}^n d\!\left(\frac{\partial f_{i_1\cdots i_k}}{\partial x^j}\right) \wedge dx^j \wedge dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}$$

Since \(d\!\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x^j}\right) = \sum_{\ell=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^\ell \partial x^j}\,dx^\ell\), we have:

$$d(d\omega) = \sum_{i_1 < \cdots < i_k} \sum_{j,\ell} \frac{\partial^2 f_{i_1\cdots i_k}}{\partial x^\ell \partial x^j}\, dx^\ell \wedge dx^j \wedge dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}$$

By the equality of mixed partial derivatives, \(\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^\ell \partial x^j} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^j \partial x^\ell}\), while wedge products anticommute: \(dx^\ell \wedge dx^j = -dx^j \wedge dx^\ell\). Each pair of terms with indices \(j, \ell\) cancels with its partner obtained by swapping \(j\) and \(\ell\). Hence all terms vanish, and \(d^2\omega = 0\). ∎
